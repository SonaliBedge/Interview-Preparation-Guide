Why Agile Exists? ü§î

The Problem with Traditional Waterfall:

Waterfall Model: Linear, sequential approach where each phase must be completed before the next begins.

  • Requirements ‚Üí Design ‚Üí Implementation ‚Üí Testing ‚Üí Deployment ‚Üí Maintenance

Problems with Waterfall:

‚ùå Late Discovery of Issues

Testing happens at the end, making bugs expensive to fix

‚ùå Inflexible to Change

Customer requirements often change, but Waterfall can't adapt easily

‚ùå Long Wait Times

Customers wait months or years to see working software

‚ùå Risk of Building Wrong Product

No feedback until the end means potential misalignment with user needs

üéØ Agile's Solution: Break work into small, manageable chunks (iterations), deliver working software frequently, and adapt based on feedback. This reduces risk and ensures we're building what users actually need!

"We are uncovering better ways of developing software by doing it and helping others do it."

12 Principles of Agile (Part 1) üìã

1. Customer Satisfaction through Early & Continuous Delivery

Our highest priority is to satisfy the customer through early and continuous delivery of valuable software.

2. Welcome Changing Requirements

Welcome changing requirements, even late in development. Agile processes harness change for the customer's competitive advantage.

3. Deliver Working Software Frequently

Deliver working software frequently, from a couple of weeks to a couple of months, with preference to shorter timescales.

4. Business & Developers Work Together Daily

Business people and developers must work together daily throughout the project.

5. Build Projects Around Motivated Individuals

Build projects around motivated individuals. Give them the environment and support they need, and trust them to get the job done.

6. Face-to-Face Conversation

The most efficient and effective method of conveying information is face-to-face conversation.

12 Principles of Agile (Part 2) üìã

7. Working Software is Primary Measure of Progress

Working software is the primary measure of progress.

8. Sustainable Development

Agile processes promote sustainable development. Sponsors, developers, and users should be able to maintain a constant pace indefinitely.

9. Technical Excellence & Good Design

Continuous attention to technical excellence and good design enhances agility.

10. Simplicity is Essential

Simplicity‚Äîthe art of maximizing the amount of work not done‚Äîis essential.

11. Self-Organizing Teams

The best architectures, requirements, and designs emerge from self-organizing teams.

12. Regular Reflection & Adjustment

At regular intervals, the team reflects on how to become more effective, then tunes and adjusts its behavior accordingly.

How Agile Works? ‚öôÔ∏è

The Iterative Cycle:

Agile works through repeated cycles called iterations or sprints (typically 1-4 weeks long). Each iteration delivers a potentially shippable product increment.

The Agile Development Cycle:

Step 1: Plan üìÖ

Team selects features from the product backlog to work on during the iteration. Work is broken down into small tasks.

Step 2: Design & Develop üíª

Team collaborates to design and build the selected features. Daily communication keeps everyone aligned.

Step 3: Test & Integrate üß™

Continuous testing ensures quality. Features are integrated frequently (sometimes multiple times per day).

Step 4: Review & Demo üëÄ

Team demonstrates working software to stakeholders and gathers feedback.

Step 5: Reflect & Improve üîÑ

Team discusses what went well, what didn't, and how to improve in the next iteration.

üîÅ Then the cycle repeats! Each iteration builds on the previous one, incorporating feedback and adapting to changes.

Common Agile Frameworks üîß

1. Scrum üèâ (Most Popular)

What it is: A framework with defined roles, ceremonies, and artifacts.

Sprint duration: 1-4 weeks (commonly 2 weeks)

Best for: Teams that need structure and clear roles

Key feature: Fixed-length sprints with specific ceremonies

2. Kanban üìä

What it is: Visual workflow management system focused on continuous delivery

Sprint duration: No sprints - continuous flow

Best for: Teams with varying priorities and continuous work streams

Key feature: Visual board with WIP (Work In Progress) limits

3. Extreme Programming (XP) üí™

What it is: Engineering-focused framework emphasizing technical practices

Sprint duration: 1-2 weeks

Best for: Teams focused on software quality and engineering excellence

Key feature: Pair programming, TDD, continuous integration

4. Lean Software Development üéØ

What it is: Adapted from Lean Manufacturing, focuses on eliminating waste

Best for: Organizations wanting to optimize efficiency

Key feature: Minimize waste, maximize value

Scrum vs Kanban Comparison üìä

Aspect Scrum Kanban
Iterations Fixed-length sprints (1-4 weeks) Continuous flow, no sprints
Roles Scrum Master, Product Owner, Dev Team No required roles
Changes No changes during sprint Can change priorities anytime
Commitment Team commits to sprint goals No required commitments
Board Board reset after each sprint Persistent board
Metrics Velocity (story points per sprint) Lead time, cycle time, throughput
Best For Complex projects needing structure Support/maintenance work with varying priorities

üí° Pro Tip: Many teams use Scrumban - a hybrid approach that combines Scrum's structure with Kanban's flexibility!

Agile Roles (Scrum Focus) üë•

1. Product Owner (PO) üìã

Responsibilities:

  • Defines and prioritizes the product backlog
  • Represents stakeholders and customers
  • Makes decisions about features and requirements
  • Accepts or rejects work results
  • Ensures team builds the right product

Key Skill: Vision, communication, decision-making

2. Scrum Master (SM) üéØ

Responsibilities:

  • Facilitates Scrum ceremonies (meetings)
  • Removes impediments blocking the team
  • Coaches team on Agile practices
  • Shields team from external distractions
  • Promotes continuous improvement

Key Skill: Facilitation, coaching, problem-solving

Note: NOT a project manager! More like a servant leader.

3. Development Team üíª

Responsibilities:

  • Self-organizing and cross-functional
  • Delivers potentially shippable increments
  • Estimates and commits to work
  • Collaborates daily
  • Collectively responsible for quality

Typical size: 5-9 people (small enough to be agile)

Key Skill: Technical expertise, collaboration, self-management

Agile Ceremonies (Scrum Events) üìÖ

1. Sprint Planning üéØ

When: Beginning of each sprint

Duration: ~2 hours per week of sprint (4 hours for 2-week sprint)

Purpose: Team selects work from backlog and creates sprint goal

Attendees: Entire Scrum team

Output: Sprint backlog and sprint goal

2. Daily Standup (Daily Scrum) ‚òÄÔ∏è

When: Every day at same time

Duration: 15 minutes max

Purpose: Synchronize work and identify blockers

Three Questions:

  • What did I do yesterday?
  • What will I do today?
  • Are there any blockers/impediments?

3. Sprint Review (Demo) üé¨

When: End of sprint

Duration: ~1 hour per week of sprint

Purpose: Demo completed work to stakeholders and get feedback

Attendees: Scrum team + stakeholders

Output: Feedback and potential backlog updates

4. Sprint Retrospective üîÑ

When: After sprint review, before next planning

Duration: ~45 minutes per week of sprint

Purpose: Team reflects on process and identifies improvements

Attendees: Scrum team only

Key Questions: What went well? What didn't? What can we improve?

5. Backlog Refinement (Grooming) ‚úÇÔ∏è

When: Mid-sprint (ongoing)

Duration: ~10% of sprint capacity

Purpose: Review, clarify, and estimate upcoming backlog items

Attendees: Product Owner + Development Team

Interview Questions & Answers üíº

Q1: What is the difference between Agile and Scrum?

Answer: Agile is a mindset and philosophy based on the Agile Manifesto and 12 principles. It's a broad approach to software development. Scrum is a specific framework that implements Agile principles with defined roles (Product Owner, Scrum Master, Dev Team), ceremonies (Sprint Planning, Daily Standup, Review, Retrospective), and artifacts (Product Backlog, Sprint Backlog, Increment). Think of it as: Agile is the "what" and "why," while Scrum is one "how" to implement Agile.

Q2: Explain the concept of a Sprint.

Answer: A Sprint is a time-boxed iteration in Scrum, typically 1-4 weeks (commonly 2 weeks), during which the team works to complete a set of planned work items from the backlog. Each Sprint has a goal, and the team delivers a potentially shippable product increment at the end. The Sprint duration is fixed and doesn't change, creating a consistent rhythm for the team. Sprints allow for regular inspection and adaptation.

Q3: What is a Product Backlog?

Answer: The Product Backlog is an ordered list of everything that might be needed in the product. It's the single source of requirements for any changes to be made to the product. The Product Owner is responsible for the Product Backlog, including its content, availability, and ordering. Items at the top are more detailed and ready for development, while lower items are less refined. The backlog is dynamic and constantly evolving as we learn more about the product and its users.

Q4: How do you handle changing requirements in Agile?

Answer: Agile welcomes changing requirements as they provide opportunities to deliver more value. During Sprint Planning, we only commit to the current sprint's work. New requirements are added to the Product Backlog and prioritized by the Product Owner. They can be incorporated in future sprints. If a requirement is urgent, we might adjust the backlog priority, but we typically don't change mid-sprint commitments. This approach balances flexibility with team focus and predictability.

Q5: What's the difference between a Scrum Master and a Project Manager?

Answer: A Scrum Master is a servant leader who facilitates the Scrum process, removes impediments, and coaches the team on Agile practices. They don't assign tasks or manage people‚Äîthe team self-organizes. A traditional Project Manager typically has more authority, manages resources, creates detailed plans, and assigns work. The Scrum Master focuses on "how" the team works (process), while the Product Owner focuses on "what" to build (product). In Agile, project management responsibilities are distributed across the entire Scrum team.
